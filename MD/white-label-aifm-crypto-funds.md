# What Is a White Label AIFM and How Does It Work for Crypto Funds?

> **Key Takeaways**
> - A white label AIFM (also called a third-party or host AIFM) lets fund managers access full EU regulatory authorization without building their own management company.
> - 55% of traditional hedge funds had digital asset exposure in 2025, up from 47% in 2024, accelerating demand for compliant crypto fund structures *(PwC/AIMA, 2025)*.
> - Luxembourg and Ireland dominate white label AIFM services; Luxembourg alone hosts 298 authorized IFMs managing EUR 7,206.3bn in assets *(CSSF, 2024)*.
> - AIFMD II (transposition deadline: 16 April 2026) tightens delegation rules and substance requirements, reshaping what white label arrangements must disclose.
> - The breakeven point for building an in-house AIFM versus using a white label service is approximately EUR 4-6bn AUM, making white label the default for most emerging crypto funds *(Langham Hall)*.

Institutional capital is flooding into crypto, yet EU regulatory requirements threaten to block smaller managers from the market entirely. The EU's alternative investment fund regime covers assets under management exceeding EUR 100 million for leveraged funds or EUR 500 million for unleveraged funds, and building a compliant in-house infrastructure can cost well over EUR 800,000 per year before a single asset is managed. The white label AIFM model solves this problem directly.

---

## What Is an AIFM?

The Alternative Investment Fund Manager Directive (AIFMD), adopted in 2011 and implemented across EU member states by 2013, created a single regulatory framework for managers of alternative investment funds. An AIFM, or Alternative Investment Fund Manager, is the legal entity that bears responsibility for portfolio management, risk management, and regulatory compliance for one or more AIFs. The EU's AIF universe held a NAV of EUR 6.8 trillion at the end of 2022 *(ESMA, 2023)*, making it one of the largest regulated investment management regimes in the world.

### Who Must Register or Authorize as an AIFM?

The directive applies differently depending on AUM thresholds. Managers of leveraged funds breaching EUR 100 million AUM, or managers of unleveraged funds breaching EUR 500 million AUM, must obtain full AIFM authorization from their home-state regulator. Managers below these thresholds can operate under a lighter "sub-threshold" or "registered AIFM" regime, with fewer obligations but limited passporting rights across EU member states.

### What Does an AIFM Actually Do?

An AIFM carries out two core functions under AIFMD: portfolio management and risk management. These functions cannot be fully delegated away, a point that becomes critical when understanding white label arrangements. The AIFM also oversees valuation, liquidity management, regulatory reporting (Annex IV reports to national competent authorities), and investor disclosure via the Alternative Investment Fund Manager (AIFM) prospectus. Minimum initial capital requirements stand at EUR 125,000 for externally managed AIFs, plus an additional 0.02% of AUM above EUR 250 million *(Linklaters; AIFMD)*.

## What Is a White Label AIFM?

A white label AIFM, also called a third-party AIFM, host AIFM, or Management Company (ManCo), is an already-authorized AIFM that makes its regulatory license available to other fund managers on a commercial basis. The fund manager, often called the investment manager or sub-advisor, plugs into the white label AIFM's existing infrastructure rather than constructing its own. The white label AIFM retains ultimate regulatory responsibility, while the investment manager handles day-to-day portfolio decisions under delegation agreements reviewed and overseen by the AIFM.

### How Does a White Label AIFM Differ from an In-House AIFM?

The core distinction is ownership and accountability. With an in-house AIFM, the fund manager builds, staffs, capitalizes, and maintains its own authorized entity, bearing all regulatory capital requirements, compliance costs, and supervisory obligations directly. With a white label AIFM, those obligations remain with the third-party host, and the fund manager pays a service fee instead. The white label AIFM must, under AIFMD rules, genuinely supervise delegated activities. It cannot simply act as a letterbox entity. Regulators scrutinize this distinction closely, particularly post-AIFMD II.

### Common Alternative Names for White Label AIFMs

Different jurisdictions and service providers use different terminology for the same concept. You'll encounter "host AIFM," "third-party AIFM," "umbrella AIFM," "ManCo services," and "AIFM platform." All refer to the same basic structure: a regulated manager-of-record providing its authorization to multiple underlying fund clients simultaneously.

## How White Label AIFMs Work

White label AIFMs operate through a carefully structured delegation and oversight model defined by AIFMD Articles 20 and 76. The AIFM formally delegates portfolio management to the investment manager (the crypto fund sponsor), but retains and actively exercises its risk management, oversight, and reporting responsibilities. Critically, the AIFM cannot delegate so much that it becomes an empty shell. Regulators require genuine "substance" in the form of qualified staff, decision-making capacity, and documented oversight procedures.

### Core Services Provided by a White Label AIFM

White label AIFMs typically provide a bundled set of services covering the full AIFMD compliance stack. These include:

- **Regulatory authorization**: The AIFM acts as the authorized manager-of-record with the relevant national competent authority (e.g., CSSF in Luxembourg, CBI in Ireland).
- **Risk management oversight**: Independent review of the investment manager's risk framework, including leverage monitoring, liquidity stress testing, and Value-at-Risk assessments.
- **Compliance and AML**: Ongoing AIFMD compliance monitoring, Anti-Money Laundering (AML) procedures, and MiFID-related investor suitability checks where applicable.
- **Regulatory reporting**: Preparation and submission of Annex IV reports, FATCA/CRS filings, and national-level regulatory returns.
- **Valuation oversight**: Independent review or approval of NAV calculation processes.
- **Investor relations support**: Preparation of marketing materials subject to national private placement rules or the AIFMD passport.
- **Depositary coordination**: Management of the mandatory depositary relationship required for all authorized AIFs.

![Diagram illustrating the white label AIFM delegation chain: investment manager, white label AIFM, depositary, and national competent authority](image-placeholder-delegation-model.jpg)

### The Delegation Model in Practice

In practice, the investment manager retains full control over portfolio construction, trading decisions, and investor relationships. The white label AIFM reviews and approves the investment strategy, monitors risk limits in real time or near-real time, and can instruct the investment manager to reduce positions if limits are breached. Legal agreements governing this relationship include the Investment Management Agreement (IMA), the Delegation Agreement, and the AIFM services agreement. The fund vehicle itself, whether a Luxembourg RAIF, Irish QIAIF, or another structure, is a separate legal entity from both the white label AIFM and the investment manager.

### Regulatory Capital Avoided

One of the most tangible benefits of the white label structure is avoiding the AIFM's own capital requirements. A manager launching a fund at EUR 200 million AUM would face minimum regulatory capital of EUR 125,000 plus 0.02% of AUM above EUR 250 million in an in-house AIFM. At EUR 1 billion AUM, that capital requirement rises to approximately EUR 275,000: capital that must be held permanently, earning little or no return. The white label model converts this fixed capital drag into a variable operating cost.

## White Label AIFMs and Crypto Funds: The Regulatory Framework

Crypto funds operating in the EU face a dual regulatory layer that makes the white label AIFM particularly valuable. Any fund investing in crypto assets above certain thresholds must comply with both AIFMD, governing the manager's authorization and conduct, and the Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA), governing the underlying crypto assets themselves. Global institutional crypto AUM grew from $36 billion at the end of 2020 to over $150 billion by the end of 2024 *(AIMA)*, yet the regulatory infrastructure to support this growth is still taking shape.

<!-- Internal link opportunity: MiCA compliance guide for fund managers -->

### The CSSF's Position on Crypto Assets in Funds

Luxembourg's Commission de Surveillance du Secteur Financier (CSSF) has published detailed guidance on how investment funds can hold crypto assets. The critical threshold is 10% of NAV: funds whose crypto asset exposure exceeds 10% of NAV require a specific CSSF authorization covering crypto-asset activities, as confirmed in the CSSF's updated position in March 2026 *(K&L Gates, 2026)*. Funds below this threshold face lighter disclosure requirements. The white label AIFM in this context must itself hold or be affiliated with an entity holding the relevant crypto-asset service authorization, or demonstrate that custody and trading arrangements comply with MiCA's requirements for crypto-asset service providers.

### MiCA's Interaction with AIFMD

MiCA, which became fully applicable in December 2024, regulates crypto-asset service providers (CASPs), including exchanges, custodians, and portfolio managers of crypto assets. A fund investing in crypto assets through MiCA-authorized CASPs benefits from a clearer regulatory perimeter. The white label AIFM must ensure that all service providers within the fund structure, from the crypto custodian to any trading counterparty, hold appropriate MiCA or transitional authorizations. This due-diligence obligation sits firmly with the AIFM, not the investment manager.

### AIFMD II: What Changes in April 2026?

AIFMD II entered into force on 15 April 2024, with member states required to transpose it into national law by 16 April 2026 *(Morgan Lewis, 2024)*. The revised directive introduces several changes that directly affect white label AIFM arrangements. New delegation reporting requirements mean AIFMs must report the total value of assets managed under delegation to their home-state regulators. Substance requirements for AIFMs have been reinforced: an AIFM must employ sufficient human resources and have adequate technical infrastructure to genuinely oversee delegated functions. Conflict-of-interest disclosure rules have been tightened, particularly relevant where a white label AIFM serves multiple competing fund clients simultaneously.

## Key Jurisdictions for Crypto Fund White Label AIFMs

Four EU and EEA jurisdictions dominate the white label AIFM market for crypto funds, each with distinct structural advantages, regulatory cultures, and cost profiles. Luxembourg remains the clear leader by volume: 298 authorized IFMs in Luxembourg manage EUR 7,206.3 billion in assets, with Luxembourg AIF NAV growing 18% in 2024 alone *(CSSF, 2024)*.

![Jurisdiction comparison map showing Luxembourg, Ireland, Liechtenstein, and Malta as leading white label AIFM centers for crypto funds](image-placeholder-jurisdictions-map.jpg)

| Jurisdiction | Primary Structure | Regulator | Crypto-Specific Rules | Passporting | Setup Timeline |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Luxembourg** | RAIF, SIF, SICAR | CSSF | CSSF crypto FAQ (>10% NAV threshold); MiCA applies | Full EU passport | 2-4 months (RAIF) |
| **Ireland** | QIAIF (ILP, ICAV, Unit Trust) | Central Bank of Ireland (CBI) | CBI guidance on crypto assets; MiCA applies | Full EU passport | 3-6 months |
| **Liechtenstein** | AIF, SIF | FMA | EEA MiCA participation; token economy regulation | EEA passport (EU member state access) | 3-5 months |
| **Malta** | AIF, NAIF | MFSA | Early mover; post-MiCA transition ongoing; MFSA crypto rules evolving | Full EU passport | 3-6 months |

### Luxembourg: The Default Choice

Luxembourg's dominance reflects decades of fund structuring expertise and a pragmatic regulatory approach. The Reserved Alternative Investment Fund (RAIF) structure, introduced in 2016, does not require direct CSSF approval of the fund itself. Only the AIFM must be authorized. This makes Luxembourg RAIFs the fastest route to market for crypto fund managers using a white label AIFM. The CSSF has also been among the more proactive EU regulators in publishing clear guidance on crypto asset exposure limits and custody requirements.

### Ireland: A Strong Alternative

Ireland's QIAIF regime, overseen by the Central Bank of Ireland, offers a competitive alternative, particularly for managers with US investor bases who value common-law legal traditions and Dublin's deep financial services ecosystem. The ICAV (Irish Collective Asset-management Vehicle) structure is particularly popular. Setup timelines tend to run slightly longer than Luxembourg RAIFs, but the CBI has developed a clear crypto asset framework and a growing cohort of white label AIFMs with digital asset expertise.

### Liechtenstein: EEA Passport with Regulatory Innovation

Liechtenstein offers an EEA passport covering all EU member states despite not being an EU member itself. The country was an early mover on blockchain and token regulation through its Blockchain Act (Token and Trusted Technology Service Provider Act), giving local white label AIFMs deep familiarity with tokenized asset structures. For crypto fund managers seeking a smaller, more bespoke regulatory environment alongside full EEA access, Liechtenstein is a credible option.

### Malta: Monitoring Post-MiCA Transition

Malta was an early mover in crypto regulation through its Virtual Financial Assets (VFA) Act, attracting significant crypto business in 2018-2020. Post-MiCA, the VFA framework is being aligned with the EU-wide regime, creating some transitional complexity. White label AIFMs in Malta are available, but managers should monitor the MFSA's ongoing MiCA implementation carefully before committing to a Maltese structure.

## Step-by-Step: Launching a Crypto Fund Under a White Label AIFM

Setting up a crypto fund using a white label AIFM in Luxembourg follows a nine-step process. Total costs typically run EUR 50,000-100,000 for RAIF setup, with ongoing annual costs depending on AUM *(Ocorian; Damalion)*. The full process from initial mandate to first close typically takes two to four months for a Luxembourg RAIF.

**Step 1: Define the Investment Strategy and Target Investor Base**
Determine the fund's crypto asset strategy (long-only, market-neutral, multi-strategy), target investor type (professional investors only for a RAIF/QIAIF), and distribution countries. This drives the choice of fund vehicle and AIFM requirements.

**Step 2: Select a White Label AIFM**
Identify white label AIFMs authorized in your target jurisdiction with documented experience managing crypto-asset funds. Verify they hold or can coordinate required MiCA-related authorizations for crypto asset service activities.

**Step 3: Conduct Legal and Regulatory Scoping**
Engage a Luxembourg law firm to confirm the fund structure, AML/KYC requirements, CSSF crypto threshold applicability, and any required MiCA compliance steps for the specific crypto assets in scope.

**Step 4: Negotiate and Execute the AIFM Services Agreement**
Agree on scope of services, fees, delegation boundaries, risk limit frameworks, and reporting cadence with the white label AIFM. The Investment Management Agreement between the AIFM and the investment manager is signed at this stage.

**Step 5: Establish the Fund Vehicle**
Incorporate the RAIF (or chosen fund vehicle) under Luxembourg law. Appoint the white label AIFM as the AIFM of record. Engage a Luxembourg-registered depositary for asset safekeeping.

**Step 6: Prepare the Offering Document**
Draft the Private Placement Memorandum (PPM) or Issuing Document in compliance with AIFMD disclosure requirements and CSSF guidance on crypto asset risk disclosures.

**Step 7: Notify the CSSF**
The white label AIFM notifies the CSSF of the new AIF under management. For RAIFs, this is a notification process, not a full fund authorization, which is a key advantage of the structure.

**Step 8: Establish Crypto Asset Infrastructure**
Onboard a MiCA-authorized or transitionally compliant crypto custodian, establish trading counterparty relationships, and implement risk management systems meeting AIFMD's operational risk requirements.

**Step 9: Launch and First Close**
Complete investor onboarding, AML/KYC verification, and execute the first close. The white label AIFM begins its ongoing supervisory and reporting obligations from the fund's launch date.

## Sub-Threshold vs Full AIFM: Which Do Crypto Funds Need?

Not every crypto fund manager needs a fully authorized AIFM or a full white label AIFM arrangement. The applicable regime depends on AUM and whether the fund uses leverage. Understanding this distinction can save significant cost and complexity, particularly for emerging managers in early fundraising stages.

| Feature | Sub-Threshold AIFM (Registered) | Full AIFM (Authorized) |
|---|---|---|
| **AUM Threshold** | Below EUR 100m (leveraged) / EUR 500m (unleveraged) | Above EUR 100m (leveraged) / EUR 500m (unleveraged) |
| **EU Passport** | No - national private placement only | Yes - full AIFMD marketing passport |
| **AIFMD Requirements** | Lighter registration; fewer ongoing obligations | Full AIFMD compliance stack |
| **Regulatory Capital** | Lower / none for registered managers | EUR 125,000 + 0.02% of AUM above EUR 250m |
| **Depositary Requirement** | Not mandatory (but recommended) | Mandatory |
| **Annex IV Reporting** | Simplified or not required | Full Annex IV reporting required |
| **White Label Option** | Sub-threshold white label AIFMs available | Full white label AIFM required |
| **Investor Access** | Limited to domestic professional investors | Pan-EU professional investors |
| **Typical Use Case** | Startup crypto funds under EUR 100m | Growth-stage funds targeting EU-wide distribution |

For most crypto fund managers with genuine EU distribution ambitions, the full authorized AIFM regime is the practical target even at lower AUM levels. The EU passport's value, which is access to professional investors across all 27 member states, typically outweighs the additional compliance cost, which the white label model makes manageable.

## Costs and the White Label vs In-House AIFM Decision

The white label AIFM model is economically dominant for all but the largest managers. An in-house AIFM requires a minimum cost floor of approximately EUR 800,000 per year, covering permanent staff (compliance officer, risk manager, operations), regulatory capital maintenance, legal costs, and technology. The breakeven point between a white label AIFM and building in-house sits at approximately EUR 4-6 billion AUM, a level very few crypto funds currently reach *(Langham Hall; Aztec Group)*.

### Cost Benchmarking: Key Line Items

For a white label AIFM arrangement at EUR 100 million AUM, total annual costs typically break down as follows. The white label AIFM service fee accounts for the largest share, typically EUR 80,000-120,000 per year depending on service scope. Legal, audit, and regulatory reporting costs add EUR 30,000-50,000. Depositary fees run EUR 15,000-30,000. Administration and NAV calculation add EUR 20,000-40,000. At EUR 100 million AUM, total annual operating costs land in the EUR 145,000-240,000 range, well below the EUR 800,000+ fixed floor of an in-house AIFM.

### When Does Building In-House Make Sense?

Beyond EUR 4-6 billion AUM, the economics begin to favor building an in-house AIFM. At this scale, the white label AIFM's percentage-of-AUM fee structure becomes more expensive than hiring dedicated compliance, risk, and regulatory staff internally. Speed and confidentiality are secondary drivers: large managers sometimes prefer an in-house AIFM for proprietary process reasons, not just cost. Most crypto fund managers launching below EUR 500 million should default to the white label model.

## Benefits of Using a White Label AIFM for Crypto Funds

[UNIQUE INSIGHT]: The white label AIFM model offers crypto fund managers a structural advantage beyond cost savings: it separates the regulatory liability layer from the investment management function, allowing managers to iterate on strategy without triggering regulatory re-authorization.

The practical benefits of the white label AIFM model for crypto funds are substantial:

1. **Speed to market.** A Luxembourg RAIF under an existing white label AIFM can launch in two to four months. Building an in-house AIFM from scratch typically takes twelve to eighteen months.

2. **Regulatory capital efficiency.** Managers avoid the permanent capital drag of EUR 125,000 plus the 0.02% AUM buffer requirement, which compounds significantly as AUM grows.

3. **EU marketing passport.** A fully authorized white label AIFM grants access to the AIFMD passport, enabling fund marketing to professional investors across all 27 EU member states from a single authorization.

4. **Established compliance infrastructure.** White label AIFMs bring ready-built AML frameworks, Annex IV reporting systems, and CSSF-approved risk management procedures. Crypto fund managers benefit immediately without building from zero.

5. **Crypto regulatory expertise.** Established white label AIFMs with crypto mandates have already navigated CSSF crypto guidance, MiCA transitional arrangements, and depositary acceptance challenges. This institutional knowledge shortens the regulatory learning curve significantly.

6. **Scalable cost structure.** White label AIFM fees scale with AUM rather than carrying high fixed costs, which is appropriate for funds in early growth phases where AUM can vary materially.

7. **Strategy flexibility.** Launching under a white label AIFM does not permanently bind the manager's regulatory structure. As AUM grows toward the EUR 4-6 billion breakeven, the manager can plan an orderly transition to an in-house AIFM without unwinding investor relationships or fund structures.

## Risks and Considerations

The white label AIFM model is not without drawbacks. Managers evaluating this route should weigh six material risks:

1. **Loss of direct regulatory control.** The AIFM is the regulated entity, not the investment manager. If the white label AIFM receives a regulatory sanction or loses its authorization, all funds under its umbrella face operational disruption. Due diligence on the AIFM's own regulatory track record is essential.

2. **Alignment of interest tensions.** A white label AIFM managing twenty or thirty funds simultaneously may deprioritize one client's urgent regulatory query. Service level agreements (SLAs) and escalation procedures must be contractually embedded.

3. **Delegation substance scrutiny.** Post-AIFMD II, regulators are increasingly focused on whether the AIFM genuinely supervises the investment manager or simply provides a regulatory letterbox. White label arrangements that do not demonstrate active oversight risk enforcement action against both the AIFM and the fund.

4. **MiCA complexity layering.** For crypto-specific funds, the MiCA compliance layer adds complexity that not all white label AIFMs are equipped to handle. Managers must verify that the chosen AIFM has demonstrable crypto-asset service competence, not just a willingness to take on crypto mandates.

5. **Reputational contagion risk.** If another fund managed by the same white label AIFM suffers a high-profile failure or regulatory action, the reputational spillover can affect all funds under the same AIFM umbrella, even where the investment strategies are completely unrelated.

6. **Fee escalation over time.** White label AIFM service agreements typically include AUM-linked fee tiers and annual cost escalators. Managers with rapid AUM growth may find the white label cost structure becoming materially more expensive faster than projected, accelerating the in-house decision timeline.

## How AIFMD II Changes the White Label AIFM Landscape

AIFMD II, which entered into force on 15 April 2024 with transposition required by 16 April 2026 *(Morgan Lewis, 2024)*, represents the most significant revision to the EU fund manager directive since its original 2013 implementation. For white label AIFM arrangements specifically, four changes carry direct operational impact.

[PERSONAL EXPERIENCE]: Compliance professionals working with Luxembourg-based white label AIFMs have noted that AIFMD II substance requirements are already being interpreted more strictly by the CSSF in pre-authorization discussions, even before the formal April 2026 transposition deadline.

### Delegation Reporting Requirements

Under AIFMD II, AIFMs must report to their home-state regulator the total value of assets managed under delegation arrangements. This reporting requirement creates a formal data trail of exactly how much portfolio management has been delegated to third-party investment managers, providing a direct transparency measure targeting letterbox AIFM structures. White label AIFMs with high delegation ratios should expect heightened regulatory scrutiny.

### Substance Requirements Reinforced

The revised directive clarifies that an AIFM must employ "at least two natural persons, either employed full-time by the AIFM or executive members or directors committed to conduct its business on a full-time basis." For white label AIFMs serving many funds, this substance requirement has long been met. For smaller or newer white label platforms, it creates a meaningful barrier to entry, effectively consolidating the market around established, well-resourced providers.

### Conflict-of-Interest Disclosures

AIFMD II requires explicit disclosure of conflicts of interest arising from delegation arrangements, particularly where the investment manager and the AIFM are affiliated or where the same AIFM manages competing strategies. For crypto funds, where trading strategies can be highly correlated, this disclosure requirement demands careful legal drafting in offering documents and AIFM agreements.

### April 2026 Deadline: Practical Implications

Member states must transpose AIFMD II by 16 April 2026. For crypto fund managers currently in the process of selecting a white label AIFM, this means the chosen provider should already be operating under AIFMD II-aligned procedures. Asking prospective white label AIFMs how they are addressing AIFMD II substance and delegation reporting requirements should be a standard part of the due diligence process.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can a crypto fund use a white label AIFM if it invests 100% in crypto assets?**

Yes, but the white label AIFM must be equipped to handle crypto-specific requirements. In Luxembourg, funds with crypto asset exposure above 10% of NAV require specific CSSF authorization covering crypto activities *(K&L Gates, 2026)*. The AIFM must ensure compliant custody through a MiCA-authorized or transitionally authorized crypto custodian. Not all white label AIFMs accept 100% crypto mandates, so verify this explicitly before engaging.

---

**What is the difference between a white label AIFM and a ManCo?**

They are functionally the same concept with different names. "ManCo" (Management Company) is the term used in Luxembourg for the legal entity structure; "white label AIFM" or "third-party AIFM" describes its commercial function. Both refer to an authorized alternative investment fund manager that provides its regulatory license and compliance infrastructure to external fund managers on a fee basis, retaining ultimate regulatory responsibility.

---

**How long does it take to launch a crypto fund using a white label AIFM?**

A Luxembourg RAIF under an existing white label AIFM typically launches in two to four months from initial mandate to first investor close *(Ocorian)*. This timeline includes fund vehicle incorporation, offering document preparation, CSSF notification, depositary onboarding, and crypto custodian setup. Ireland's QIAIF takes slightly longer, typically three to six months, due to Central Bank of Ireland review processes.

---

**What does a white label AIFM cost for a crypto fund?**

Total costs at EUR 100 million AUM run approximately EUR 150,000 per year, covering the AIFM service fee, depositary fees, legal, audit, and administration. At EUR 500 million AUM, expect approximately EUR 400,000 per year. These figures compare favorably to the EUR 800,000+ fixed annual cost of operating an in-house AIFM. RAIF setup costs run EUR 50,000-100,000 one-time *(Ocorian; Langham Hall)*.

---

**Do you need a white label AIFM if your crypto fund is below EUR 100 million AUM?**

Not necessarily. Funds managed by sub-threshold (registered) AIFMs face lighter AIFMD obligations and do not require a fully authorized white label AIFM. However, sub-threshold AIFMs cannot use the AIFMD marketing passport, limiting distribution to national private placement regimes only. Fund managers targeting EU-wide professional investor distribution will need a fully authorized AIFM regardless of AUM, making the white label model the practical solution at most fund sizes.

---

**How does AIFMD II affect existing white label AIFM arrangements?**

AIFMD II, with transposition required by 16 April 2026 *(Morgan Lewis, 2024)*, requires white label AIFMs to report total delegated AUM to regulators, demonstrate genuine operational substance, and make explicit conflict-of-interest disclosures. Existing arrangements structured before April 2024 should be reviewed against these new requirements. White label AIFMs have generally been proactive in updating service agreements and governance frameworks ahead of the transposition deadline.

---

**What is a RAIF and why is it commonly used with white label AIFMs?**

A Reserved Alternative Investment Fund (RAIF) is a Luxembourg fund vehicle introduced in 2016 that does not require direct CSSF authorization of the fund itself. Only the AIFM must be authorized. This makes RAIFs the fastest and most cost-effective Luxembourg fund structure for managers using a white label AIFM, since fund-level regulatory review is bypassed. RAIFs are restricted to well-informed or professional investors and can invest in any asset class including crypto assets.

---

**Is a white label AIFM suitable for a family office launching a crypto fund?**

Yes, and it is often the recommended route. Family offices typically lack the in-house regulatory expertise and compliance infrastructure to operate as their own AIFM. A white label AIFM provides the full EU regulatory framework, allowing the family office's investment team to focus on portfolio management. The EUR 50,000-100,000 RAIF setup cost and EUR 150,000-400,000 annual operating cost at sub-EUR 500 million AUM is manageable relative to typical family office asset bases *(Ocorian; Langham Hall)*.

---

## Conclusion

The white label AIFM model has become the standard entry point for crypto fund managers seeking EU regulatory authorization. It provides access to the full AIFMD framework, including the EU marketing passport, MiCA compliance coordination, and CSSF-approved risk management infrastructure, without the EUR 800,000+ annual fixed cost of building an in-house structure. For emerging and mid-size crypto funds managing below EUR 4-6 billion AUM, the economics are unambiguous.

The regulatory environment is not standing still. AIFMD II's April 2026 transposition deadline is reshaping how white label arrangements must demonstrate substance and transparency. MiCA's full application since December 2024 has added a new compliance layer that requires crypto-specific expertise from the AIFM itself. Managers evaluating white label partners should prioritize proven crypto-asset competence alongside established regulatory standing.

The growth context supports continued expansion. Institutional crypto AUM grew from $36 billion at end-2020 to over $150 billion by end-2024 *(AIMA)*, and tokenized fund AUM is projected to grow at a 41% CAGR to $715 billion by 2030 *(PwC, 2025)*. With 55% of traditional hedge funds now reporting digital asset exposure *(PwC/AIMA, 2025)*, the demand for compliant, scalable crypto fund infrastructure is structural rather than cyclical.

The practical next steps for a crypto fund manager evaluating this route are straightforward: assess whether your target AUM clears the sub-threshold boundaries, select a white label AIFM with documented crypto-asset experience, engage a Luxembourg or Irish law firm for structuring advice, and begin the two-to-four-month setup process. The regulatory infrastructure is mature, the service providers are established, and the window to position ahead of the next wave of institutional allocation is open now.
