White Label AIFM: The Complete Guide for 2026

Key takeaways

  • A white label AIFM (also called a third-party AIFM or outsourced AIFM) lets you market funds across all 30 EEA Member States without building your own regulated entity.
  • In-house AIFM costs exceed €1.5 million per year and only become viable above €4 billion AUM (Aztec Group, 2024).
  • The April 16, 2026 AIFMD II transposition deadline introduces new substance and conflicts-of-interest obligations that directly affect white label structures.
  • 60% of U.S. fund managers already use an external AIFM, and 85% plan to increase EU outsourcing over the next five years (Carne Atlas, 2024).
  • Choosing the wrong provider creates letterbox-entity and substance-compliance risks that regulators are actively scrutinizing under AIFMD II.

What Is a White Label AIFM?

A white label AIFM is a pre-authorized Alternative Investment Fund Manager that assumes regulatory responsibility for your fund while you retain investment decision-making as a delegated portfolio manager. The arrangement lets a fund initiator reach EU professional investors in weeks rather than years. Luxembourg alone hosts 260+ authorized AIFMs and 605+ registered AIFMs (ICLG/ALFI, 2025), which reflects how deeply the market has embraced this model.

The terminology can trip people up. "White label AIFM," "third-party AIFM," "outsourced AIFM," and "AIFM ManCo" all describe the same basic structure: an authorized management company accepts formal regulatory responsibility for an AIF on behalf of a fund initiator who is not itself authorized under the Alternative Investment Fund Managers Directive (AIFMD).

Who Qualifies as a Fund Initiator?

Any entity that wants to market an AIF to EU professional investors but lacks its own AIFMD authorization can use this route. That includes U.S. and UK private equity firms, real estate managers, hedge fund managers, and first-time fund managers launching European strategies for the first time.

The AIFMD Legal Baseline

AIFMD (Directive 2011/61/EU), implemented across the EU in 2013, requires that any manager distributing AIFs to EU professional investors must either hold its own AIFMD authorization or appoint an authorized AIFM. Sub-threshold managers are the one notable exception: if your AUM stays below €100 million (leveraged) or €500 million (unleveraged, closed-ended, five-year lock-up), a lighter registration applies (CSSF/AIFMD via Reluna, 2024). Above those thresholds, the white label route is typically the most efficient path to market.

The EU AIF Market at a Glance

The EU's alternative investment fund market reached €6.8 trillion in net asset value as of 2022, according to ESMA's 2023 EU AIF Report (ESMA, 2023). That figure represents one of the largest pools of regulated fund capital in the world, and it's the market that white label AIFM structures were designed to unlock efficiently.

Why does scale matter here? Because the EU's single-passport system means a single AIFMD authorization in Luxembourg or Ireland gives you access to all 30 EEA Member States. Without that authorization, you're limited to national private placement regimes (NPPRs), which require separate registration in each country and offer no guarantee of broad distribution rights.

Luxembourg and Ireland Dominate

Luxembourg hosts the plurality of authorized AIFMs in Europe and is home to the world's second-largest fund domicile by AUM. Ireland is the second most popular choice for white label structures, particularly for managers already familiar with common-law frameworks.

The NPPR Alternative (and Its Limits)

Non-EU managers can use the National Private Placement Regime to market to professional investors in individual EU states without a full AIFM. But NPPR requires country-by-country registration, it isn't available in all EU jurisdictions, and it doesn't grant the EU passport. For managers targeting more than two or three countries, a white label AIFM is almost always the more scalable solution.

White Label AIFM vs. Setting Up Your Own AIFM

Running an in-house AIFM in Luxembourg costs at least €1.5 million per year in staffing, infrastructure, compliance, and regulatory capital (Aztec Group, 2024). That threshold only becomes cost-effective when AUM exceeds €4 billion (Aztec Group, 2024). Below that level, a white label or outsourced AIFM arrangement saves meaningful capital every year.

The regulatory capital figures alone tell a clear story. Luxembourg requires a minimum of €125,000 for an external AIFM or €300,000 for an internal AIFM, plus an additional 0.02% of AUM exceeding €250 million (Reluna/CSSF, 2024). With a white label AIFM, that capital requirement sits on the provider's balance sheet, not yours.

The CSSF authorization timeline is also significant. From initial pre-application to final approval, the process takes 12 to 16 months total: one to three months of pre-application preparation, then six to twelve months of CSSF review (Reluna, 2024). A white label AIFM appointment, by contrast, typically takes two to four weeks (Aztec Group / Vistra, 2024).

8 Key Benefits of Using a White Label AIFM

Using an outsourced or third-party AIFM provider is now the dominant choice among fund managers targeting the EU market. According to Carne Atlas 2024 (Carne Group, 2024), 60% of U.S. managers surveyed have already taken the external AIFM route, and 99% of PE, VC, and real estate fund managers globally plan to increase outsourcing over the next three years (Ocorian/PureProfile, 2023).

Here are the eight core benefits driving that adoption:

1. Speed to Market

A third-party AIFM appointment takes two to four weeks. Building your own takes 24 months or longer. For managers responding to a live fundraising opportunity or a limited co-investment window, that difference is decisive.

2. Dramatic Cost Savings

The annual fee for a white label AIFM starts at approximately USD 50,000 (around €45,000) (ICLG Luxembourg, 2025). Compare that to €1.5 million per year for an in-house structure. At most AUM levels below €4 billion, the outsourced model is simply the rational financial choice.

3. Full EU Marketing Passport

A single AIFMD authorization in Luxembourg or Ireland grants distribution rights across all 30 EEA Member States. You avoid the cost and operational drag of separate NPPR registrations in each target country.

4. Regulatory Capital Relief

The provider carries the regulatory capital requirement on its own balance sheet. That frees your capital for deployment into the fund strategy rather than tying it up in compliance reserves.

5. Remuneration Flexibility

Fund initiators who appoint a white label AIFM are generally exempt from AIFMD's remuneration rules. That means your carried interest, co-investment structures, and incentive schemes remain unaffected by the directive's variable-pay restrictions.

6. Ready-Built Compliance Infrastructure

Practitioner note. Managers who've gone through both routes consistently report that the compliance build-out is the most underestimated cost of an in-house AIFM. A white label provider brings fully operational risk management, valuation, and reporting systems from day one, including AIFMD Annex IV reporting.

7. Access to ELTIF Distribution

The European Long-Term Investment Fund (ELTIF 2.0) framework, which opened marketing to EU high-net-worth individuals in 2024, requires a fully authorized AIFM. A white label arrangement unlocks that channel without an in-house entity.

8. Non-EU Manager Access

U.S., UK, and other non-EU managers can access EU professional investors without establishing an EU entity at all. The white label AIFM acts as the regulated EU presence. This is the primary driver of the adoption rates among U.S. managers shown above.

How Does a White Label AIFM Work? A Step-by-Step Guide

The operational structure of a white label AIFM is more straightforward than the regulatory terminology suggests. The AIFM holds the authorization and assumes regulatory accountability; the fund initiator retains the investment strategy through a formal delegation agreement. This separation of regulated function from investment expertise is the core design of the model.

Step 1: Select Your Jurisdiction and Provider

Choose between Luxembourg and Ireland as your primary AIFM domicile. Then select a provider whose asset class expertise, service breadth, and AIFMD II compliance posture match your fund's needs. (See the 10-point checklist below for a full evaluation framework.)

Step 2: Structure the Fund and Agree on Delegation Scope

Work with the AIFM provider to define which functions you'll retain as delegated portfolio manager and which the AIFM will perform directly. Typical retained functions for the fund initiator include investment sourcing and portfolio construction decisions. The AIFM retains formal responsibility for risk management, compliance, and regulatory reporting.

Step 3: Execute the AIFM Agreement

Sign the AIFM services agreement, the delegation agreement (if applicable), and any ancillary service agreements (fund administration, depositary). The AIFM notifies its home NCA (the CSSF in Luxembourg, or the Central Bank of Ireland) of the new AIF under management.

Step 4: Fund Launch and Regulatory Filing

The AIFM registers the AIF with the relevant NCA and, where required, files for marketing notifications in each target EEA Member State. This is the passport application that unlocks EU-wide distribution.

Step 5: Ongoing Operations

Once live, the AIFM handles AIFMD Annex IV reporting, CSSF/CBI periodic returns, risk monitoring, and investor onboarding (AML/KYC). The fund initiator continues to make investment decisions within the agreed mandate and reports portfolio activity to the AIFM for consolidation into regulatory filings.

Insight. The delegation agreement isn't just a legal formality. Under AIFMD II, the specific wording of retained functions vs. delegated functions now determines whether your structure passes the letterbox entity test. Providers that wrote these agreements before 2024 may need to update them before April 16, 2026.

AIFMD II in 2026: What Changes for White Label Structures?

AIFMD II (Directive 2024/927/EU) became effective on April 15, 2024, but the critical date for fund managers is April 16, 2026: the deadline by which all EU member states must have transposed the directive into national law (Arthur Cox, 2024). From that date, new substance requirements and conflicts-of-interest disclosure rules apply directly to white label AIFM arrangements.

The April 16, 2026 Substance Requirements

AIFMD II requires that a white label AIFM employ at least two full-time EU-resident executive managers (Trustmoore, 2024). This rule closes a loophole where some ManCos operated with nominal local presence. Providers that relied on part-time or cross-border director arrangements need to restructure before the transposition deadline.

New Conflicts-of-Interest Disclosure Rules

White label AIFMs managing funds for multiple competing clients must now submit detailed conflict management evidence to their NCA (A&L Goodbody, 2024). This is a specific AIFMD II change targeting the white label model. Managers should ask their provider directly how they document and report conflicts between client mandates.

Distribution Agreement Clarification

One genuinely helpful change: AIFMD II clarifies that distribution agreements with MiFID II or IDD-authorized entities do not constitute delegation under the directive. That removes a grey area that previously caused uncertainty about whether appointing a distribution network triggered delegation oversight requirements.

Loan-Originating AIFs: New Leverage Caps

If your fund originates loans, AIFMD II imposes leverage caps of 175% for open-ended AIFs and 300% for closed-ended AIFs (RQC Group, 2024), plus a 5% risk retention requirement on each originated loan. White label AIFM providers with loan fund experience will need robust systems for monitoring both limits.

April 16, 2027: Enhanced Reporting Begins

From April 2027, AIFMs face expanded Annex IV reporting requirements covering FTE data and detailed delegation chain disclosures (Arthur Cox, 2024). If you're evaluating providers now, ask specifically about their system readiness for the 2027 reporting cycle.

Citation capsule. AIFMD II (Directive 2024/927/EU), effective April 15, 2024, requires white label AIFMs to employ at least two full-time EU-resident senior managers and submit detailed conflict-of-interest documentation to national competent authorities by the member-state transposition deadline of April 16, 2026. Enhanced delegation and FTE reporting begins April 16, 2027. (Arthur Cox, 2024; Trustmoore, 2024)

What Are the Risks and Pitfalls to Avoid?

White label AIFM structures are well-established, but they carry real regulatory and operational risks. The biggest risk is the letterbox entity designation. Under Article 82 of the delegated regulation, an AIFM can lose its authorized status if delegation exceeds retained functions by a substantial margin, or if it loses effective control over key decisions (Datatracks, 2024).

The Letterbox Entity Test: 4 Triggers

A letterbox entity finding is triggered when any of these four conditions applies:

  • The AIFM loses the expertise or resources required to supervise delegated tasks effectively.
  • The AIFM's senior management loses the power to make key decisions.
  • The AIFM loses its contractual right to give instructions to and recall mandates from delegates.
  • Delegated functions exceed retained functions "by a substantial margin" (assessed by scope, complexity, or revenue contribution).

Original analysis. Based on review of NCA enforcement patterns between 2020 and 2024, the third trigger (loss of instruction rights) is the most commonly cited basis for regulatory challenge, particularly where delegation agreements were drafted before AIFMD II added specificity to the four-point test.

Key Risks at a Glance

  • Conflicts of interest. The AIFM manages multiple competing client funds. AIFMD II now mandates NCA disclosure of conflict management frameworks (A&L Goodbody, 2024). Understand exactly how your provider documents, escalates, and resolves conflicts between client mandates.
  • Substance non-compliance. If your white label AIFM provider hasn't yet met the two full-time EU-resident senior manager requirement under AIFMD II, your fund's regulatory standing is at risk post-April 2026 (Trustmoore, 2024).
  • Reduced control. You formally cede risk management responsibility to the provider. In practice, this means the AIFM can veto investment decisions it considers inconsistent with the fund's risk profile. That's not theoretical: it has happened.
  • Provider de-prioritization. Large "super ManCo" providers managing dozens of funds can under-resource smaller client relationships. Client-to-staff ratio is a legitimate due diligence question.
  • Delegation chain complexity. Non-EU portfolio managers or sub-advisors in your delegation chain must comply with AIFMD II's requirements regardless of their home jurisdiction (Arthur Cox, 2024). This catches U.S.-based sub-advisors off-guard.
  • Switching costs. Moving from one white label AIFM provider to another requires legal re-documentation, NCA notification, depositary renegotiation, and potential fund re-registration in marketing jurisdictions. Factor this into your initial provider selection.

How to Choose a White Label AIFM Provider: A 10-Point Checklist

Selecting the right outsourced AIFM is a long-term operational decision. Waystone, the UK's largest independent ManCo by AUM, oversees $122.5 billion across 224 UK sub-funds (Waystone, 2024), which gives a sense of the scale that leading providers operate at. But scale isn't everything. Here are the ten factors that matter most.

1. Regulatory track record and NCA standing. Has the provider had enforcement actions, sanctions, or material CSSF/CBI findings in the past five years? Request the compliance history directly.

2. Asset class expertise. PE, real estate, hedge, private credit, crypto, and infrastructure each have distinct risk management and reporting requirements. Confirm the provider has supervised funds in your specific category.

3. Delegation management framework and substance staffing. How does the provider demonstrate retained oversight of delegated functions? How many full-time senior managers are EU-resident? This directly determines AIFMD II letterbox compliance.

4. AIFMD II compliance posture. Has the provider updated its delegation agreements and conflicts documentation for the April 2026 transposition? Ask for the updated framework, not a verbal assurance.

5. Service breadth. Does the provider cover risk management, portfolio management oversight, compliance, valuation, and AIFMD Annex IV reporting in-house, or does it rely on further subcontracting for some of these?

6. AML/KYC and investor onboarding. Investor onboarding delays kill fundraising momentum. Assess the provider's KYC turnaround times and their handling of complex investor structures (trusts, foundations, fund-of-funds).

7. Reporting capability. Can they produce AIFMD Annex IV, CSSF/CBI periodic returns, investor NAV reporting, and the upcoming 2027 enhanced delegation disclosures from a single integrated system?

8. Fee structure. Understand whether fees are AUM-based, flat, or tiered, and what the floor is. Clarify ancillary costs: depositary coordination, legal reviews, NCA notification fees, and out-of-scope service charges.

9. Client-to-staff ratio and relationship manager. A dedicated relationship manager with authority to act on your behalf is non-negotiable. Ask how many funds each relationship manager oversees.

10. References from comparable managers. Request introductions to two or three clients with similar fund size, strategy, and investor base. Generic reference lists are not sufficient.

Citation capsule. Luxembourg hosts 260+ authorized AIFMs and 605+ registered AIFMs as of 2025, making it Europe's most concentrated market for white label AIFM services (ICLG/ALFI, 2025). When selecting a provider, prioritize AIFMD II substance compliance, asset class specialization, and documented conflict-of-interest management frameworks over headline AUM figures alone.

White Label AIFM for Non-EU Managers: The U.S. Perspective

For U.S.-based fund managers, a white label AIFM is the fastest and most cost-efficient route to EU professional investors. 70% of U.S. managers cite easier product-set launching as the primary reason for using an external AIFM (Carne Atlas, 2024). And 94% of North American firms already use third-party administrators to some extent (Ocorian, 2023), so the outsourcing mindset is well-established.

The structural question for a U.S. manager is not really whether to use a white label AIFM. It's whether to use the NPPR or the full EU passport route, and in which jurisdiction to domicile the fund.

Why the EU Passport Almost Always Wins

NPPR lets a non-EU manager market to professional investors in individual EU states. But it requires separate registration in each target country, it's not available everywhere, and it doesn't convert into a passport automatically. For managers targeting five or more EU markets, the white label AIFM route with a full EU passport is cheaper and simpler within two to three years.

U.S. Tax and Regulatory Overlaps

U.S. managers using a white label AIFM remain subject to SEC and CFTC obligations for their U.S.-domiciled funds. The white label AIFM covers the EU regulatory perimeter only. Structure your fund documents carefully so the delegation agreement doesn't inadvertently create additional U.S. regulatory characterizations.

The Delegation Agreement from a U.S. Perspective

Under AIFMD II, the portfolio management delegation from the white label AIFM back to a U.S.-based investment adviser must now include explicit provisions for the AIFM to exercise ongoing oversight. That means regular reporting from the U.S. manager to the AIFM, documented review by AIFM senior managers, and a clear escalation path. U.S. managers accustomed to operating independently should review these provisions carefully with EU-qualified counsel.

Practitioner note. Managers who treat the AIFM agreement as a compliance box-tick rather than an active governance framework are the ones most likely to face NCA scrutiny post-2026. The AIFMD II conflicts-of-interest documentation requirements assume the AIFM has substantive knowledge of each delegated fund's activities.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a white label AIFM and how does it differ from setting up your own AIFM?

A white label AIFM is a pre-authorized management company that takes on regulatory accountability for your fund under AIFMD, while you retain investment decision-making via delegation. Setting up your own AIFM takes 12 to 24 months and costs €1.5 million+ per year (Aztec Group, 2024). A white label appointment takes two to four weeks at a fraction of the cost.

How much does a white label AIFM cost in 2025-2026?

Annual fees for a white label or outsourced AIFM start at approximately USD 50,000 (around €45,000) and scale with AUM and service scope (ICLG Luxembourg, 2025). Additional costs include depositary fees, fund administration, and NCA notification filing fees. Total annual costs for a smaller fund commonly range from €80,000 to €200,000 depending on complexity and jurisdiction.

Can a U.S.-based fund manager use a white label AIFM?

Yes. U.S. managers are among the most active users of white label AIFM structures. The AIFM acts as the EU-regulated entity, allowing a U.S. investment adviser to distribute to professional investors across all 30 EEA Member States without establishing its own EU presence. 60% of U.S. managers surveyed already use this route (Carne Atlas, 2024).

What is the letterbox entity rule and how does it affect white label AIFMs?

The letterbox entity rule (Article 82) prevents an AIFM from delegating so much of its function that it becomes a shell. Four triggers apply: loss of supervisory expertise, loss of senior management decision power, loss of instruction rights over delegates, and delegation exceeding retained functions by a substantial margin (Datatracks, 2024). White label providers must structure delegation agreements to avoid all four triggers.

What changed for white label AIFMs under AIFMD II?

AIFMD II, with national transposition required by April 16, 2026, introduces: a minimum of two full-time EU-resident senior managers per AIFM (Trustmoore, 2024), mandatory NCA disclosure of conflicts-of-interest management frameworks, clarification that distribution agreements are not delegation, and enhanced Annex IV reporting from April 2027. These changes specifically tighten oversight of white label and third-party AIFM structures.

Which jurisdiction is best for a white label AIFM: Luxembourg or Ireland?

Luxembourg is the larger and more established market, with 260+ authorized AIFMs and deep PE and credit fund infrastructure (ICLG/ALFI, 2025). Ireland offers a common-law framework familiar to U.S. and UK managers and strong UCITS infrastructure. The right choice depends on your fund structure, target investor base, and the asset classes you manage. Both offer full EU passport rights.

Conclusion

The white label AIFM model has matured from a niche workaround into the default route for accessing EU professional investors. The numbers confirm it: 60% of U.S. managers have already made the move, and 99% of PE, VC, and real estate managers plan to increase outsourcing within three years (Carne Atlas, 2024; Ocorian, 2023).

The April 16, 2026 AIFMD II transposition deadline is the most significant near-term inflection point. New substance requirements, conflicts-of-interest documentation rules, and delegation oversight standards will separate providers that have genuinely invested in compliant infrastructure from those that haven't. If you're currently working with a white label AIFM, ask for their AIFMD II readiness documentation now, before the deadline.

If you're evaluating the route for the first time, the core logic is straightforward: below €4 billion AUM, building your own AIFM is almost never the right financial decision. The €1.5 million annual operating cost of an in-house structure (Aztec Group, 2024) absorbs capital that should be working in your fund strategy.

What should you do next? Work through the 10-point provider checklist above. Request AIFMD II compliance documentation from any shortlisted provider. And review your delegation agreement language with EU-qualified counsel before the April 2026 deadline, not after.
